This story follows five of those icons — where they live, why they are biologically unusual, and how secure their futures really are.

01 A country known by its animals

The five animals everyone draws when they draw Australia

Ask anyone to picture Australia and they will sketch the same handful of creatures. The kangaroo on the coat of arms. The koala in the eucalyptus. The duck-billed platypus. The flightless emu. These animals are not just wildlife — they are how the country presents itself to the world.

The five species in this story have together generated nearly 900,000 verified observation records in the Atlas of Living Australia — the country's national biodiversity database. They are by some margin the most-recorded terrestrial vertebrates in Australia.

That popularity is not accidental. Each carries cultural weight far beyond its biology: the kangaroo and emu appear on the national coat of arms, the platypus features on the 20-cent coin, and the koala is the international face of Australian tourism.

02 Why these animals are unique

They are unusual on a global scale

These animals are unusual not because Australia chose them, but because Australia was geographically isolated for tens of millions of years. That isolation produced two whole mammal lineages — marsupials and monotremes — that survived almost nowhere else.

When the continents were one, mammals were evolving everywhere. But when Australia broke away from Gondwana roughly 45 million years ago, it took its wildlife with it — and shut the door. While placental mammals came to dominate every other continent, Australia kept two ancient experiments alive: pouched mammals that raise their young in a pouch, and egg-laying mammals that defy almost every rule of what a mammal is supposed to do. Nearly half of all marsupial species on Earth live here, and four of the five monotremes exist nowhere else.

Three lineages, shown against their global species counts. Australia hosts a disproportionately large share of the world's pouched and egg-laying mammals.

Body weight, lifespan, days to birth or hatching, and offspring per year — four dimensions that show just how different these five animals really are.

The biology makes the point even more sharply. The platypus lays eggs, detects prey through electric fields in the water, and carries a venomous spur on its hind leg — a combination found in no other mammal on Earth. The red kangaroo carries a joey the size of a jellybean at birth, then nurses it in a pouch for months while the animal learns to exist. The emu cannot fly, stands taller than most humans, and leaves the male to incubate the eggs alone. None of this is typical. All of it is Australian.

03 Different animals, different Australias

No single Australia holds all five

The five icons share a flag but not a landscape. Each species has carved out its own version of Australia — defined not by borders but by rainfall, vegetation, and water. Plot them all at once and the continent dissolves into five overlapping territories.

Every dot is a real sighting. Click a species in the legend to isolate its range.

The separation is striking even at a glance. Platypus dots cluster tightly along the east-coast river systems — they need clean, flowing water and cannot survive in the arid centre. Koala dots hug the same coast but sit further inland, tracing the eucalypt forests. Red kangaroo dots spread across the vast, dry interior where no koala could live. Emu dots appear almost everywhere — the most adaptable of the five, at home from the coast to the outback. Use the zoom to see how the pattern sharpens inside each state.

Each ~50 km cell coloured by the species recorded there most often. Hover a cell to read all five counts.

Each colour boundary is an ecological boundary — where rainfall drops, the koala's range ends and the red kangaroo's begins.

Share of each state's observation records by species. Bars sorted by total records — NSW leads because it is Australia's most densely monitored state.

NSW leads not because it has the most wildlife, but the most observers. South Australia is red kangaroo country. Tasmania stands completely apart — no wild kangaroos, no koalas, overwhelmingly platypus and emu.

04 Famous, but not always safe

Cultural fame does not protect them

Of the five species in this story, two are quietly disappearing. Koalas were uplisted from Vulnerable to Endangered in February 2022 across Queensland, NSW and the ACT. Platypuses are classified Near Threatened internationally, and their numbers are falling almost everywhere they live.

Each card shows the worst status across the IUCN global list and Australia's EPBC Act, and the current population trend.

The 2019–20 Black Summer was the moment the numbers became undeniable. In a single fire season, an estimated 80,000+ koalas were lost across eastern Australia. Kangaroo Island alone lost 80% of its koala population. But behind the headline fires, the daily pressures are just as relentless — land clearing, disease, river regulation, drought.

Left bars = hectares burnt (thousands); right bars = estimated koalas lost. South Australia's outsized toll reflects Kangaroo Island, where 4 in 5 koalas were lost.

In 10 years the koala has moved 2 steps

from Vulnerable (2012) to Endangered (2022) under the EPBC Act — not because the laws tightened, but because the data forced them to.

South Australia's number looks extreme: Kangaroo Island, home to a genetically distinct koala population, lost an estimated 4 in 5 animals in a single season.

Fire is the most visible threat — but not the only one. Every day, each species faces a different set of pressures shaped by where it lives and how it reproduces. The koala is exposed on almost every front: its dependence on a single tree species makes habitat clearing catastrophic, its slow breeding rate means disease can spread faster than recovery, and its visibility in suburban areas turns roads into a constant hazard. The platypus faces a quieter crisis — dams, water extraction and drought silently shrink the rivers it needs to survive.

Threat severity per species — Low / Medium / High. Grey cells = not listed in current conservation advice. Click a cell to highlight its row and column.

One pattern stands out across the whole grid: habitat clearing and vehicle strike are the only two threats that affect every single species at medium or high severity. Roads and land use are Australia-wide problems with no easy fix — and they fall equally on the vulnerable and the abundant.

05 Protecting the icons, protecting Australia

Attention has grown — but attention is not protection

Since Black Summer, Australians have been watching more closely. Koala sightings reported to the Atlas of Living Australia grew nearly tenfold in the years after the fires. Platypus records rose sharply too. It is one of the more hopeful numbers in this story — and one of the more easily misread. More observations do not mean more animals. They mean more people paying attention to animals that were already there.

Annual observations indexed to 2015. Rising lines mean more eyes on the species — not more animals.

The chart above shows effort, not abundance. What it cannot show is whether the attention translates into action — whether the habitat gets protected, the rivers kept flowing, the forests left standing. That is a different kind of record-keeping entirely. The map below brings all five species together one last time. Three circles are green. Two are not. The gap between them opened in a generation. Whether it closes depends on what happens next.

Bubble size = total observations. Colour = conservation status. Two species are slipping — and both are declining.

Australia's iconic animals are still here. The kangaroos still hop, the emus still stride, the platypuses still dive. But the koala moved from Vulnerable to Endangered in ten years — not slowly, not quietly, but in plain sight. The platypus is following a similar trajectory, reach by reach, river by river. Protecting the icons is not really about animals. It is about whether Australia keeps the forests, rivers and grasslands that gave those animals a reason to exist here at all — and that gave Australia a reason to put them on its coins and its coat of arms in the first place.
